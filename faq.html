Climate Zones

What are climate zones?

Climate zones are geographic regions with similar climate characteristics. For building purposes, these zones help determine appropriate construction techniques, insulation requirements, and energy efficiency standards.

Our application uses two main classification systems:

  • IECC Zones: The International Energy Conservation Code divides the US into climate zones numbered 1-8 (hottest to coldest) with moisture designations (A=humid, B=dry, C=marine).
  • Build America Zones: A simplified system used by the US Department of Energy that groups regions into descriptive categories like "Hot-Humid", "Mixed-Dry", etc.

How are these zones determined?

Climate zones are primarily determined using Heating Degree Days (HDD) and Cooling Degree Days (CDD):

  • HDD: Measure how much and for how long outside air temperature falls below a base temperature (usually 65¬∞F/18¬∞C). Higher HDD means more heating is needed.
  • CDD: Measure how much and for how long outside air temperature rises above a base temperature. Higher CDD means more cooling is needed.

IECC zones also incorporate precipitation data to determine moisture designations (A, B, or C).

Energy Cost Calculations

How are heating costs calculated?

  • HDD: Heating Degree Days for the selected location

  • Efficiency: Heating system efficiency factor
  • Fuel Price: Cost per unit of the selected fuel type

For electric resistance heating, the efficiency is typically 100%. For heat pumps, we use the location's climate to determine an appropriate seasonal coefficient of performance (COP) ranging from 2.0 to 3.5 depending on the climate zone.

How are cooling costs calculated?

Cooling costs follow a similar principle but include additional factors for latent loads (humidity):

Cooling Energy = (CDD √ó 24 √ó UA √ó SHR / SEER) √ó Electricity Price

Where:

  • CDD: Cooling Degree Days for the selected location
  • UA: Overall heat transfer coefficient √ó area
  • SHR: Sensible Heat Ratio (accounts for latent cooling loads)
  • SEER: Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio of cooling equipment
  • Electricity Price: Local electricity cost per kWh

SHR values are adjusted based on climate moisture conditions, with more humid locations having lower SHR values (requiring more energy for dehumidification).

What are the R-value assumptions?

R-values measure thermal resistance - higher values mean better insulation. Our calculator uses the following assumptions:

  • Wall R-values:
    • R-13: Basic 2√ó4 wall with fiberglass batt insulation
    • R-19: Standard 2√ó6 wall with fiberglass batt insulation
    • R-21: 2√ó6 wall with high-density insulation
    • R-30: Advanced wall system (typically includes continuous exterior insulation)
  • Ceiling/Roof R-values:
    • R-30: Basic attic insulation (minimum code in warmer zones)
    • R-38: Better attic insulation (typical code requirement)
    • R-49: Advanced attic insulation (recommended for cold climates)
    • R-60: Premium attic insulation (optimal for very cold climates)

Window and Air Infiltration Assumptions

The calculator uses the following window and air infiltration assumptions:

  • Window U-factors:
    • Single-pane: U-1.0 (poorest insulation)
    • Double-pane: U-0.5 (standard)
    • Low-E Double-pane: U-0.35 (energy efficient)
    • Triple-pane: U-0.25 (high performance)
  • Air Infiltration Rates:
    • Tight: 0.1-0.2 ACH (Air Changes per Hour) - new/sealed construction
    • Average: 0.3-0.5 ACH - typical construction
    • Leaky: 0.6-1.0 ACH - older homes

Window area is assumed to be 15% of the floor area, distributed equally on all four sides of the home.
